What Happens After an FIR?

A practical guide to understanding what happens after a First Information Report is registered under Indian criminal law.

JJ

By Jedidiah Jsk

Apr 2026 • 10 min read

There are very few moments in life that create as much uncertainty as learning that a First Information Report (FIR) has been registered. For many people, the term itself is intimidating. Television dramas, social media discussions, and hearsay often create the impression that the moment an FIR is filed, an arrest becomes inevitable and the legal process is already decided.

The reality is very different.

An FIR is not a declaration of guilt. It is simply the legal mechanism that sets the criminal justice system in motion. It informs the police that information regarding the commission of a cognizable offence has been received and that an investigation must begin.

Understanding what happens after an FIR helps both complainants and accused persons appreciate their rights, responsibilities, and the legal process that follows.

What is an FIR?

A First Information Report is the first information recorded by the police regarding the commission of a cognizable offence. It is registered under Section 173 of the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023 (formerly Section 154 of the Code of Criminal Procedure).

The purpose of an FIR is not to determine who is guilty. Instead, it formally authorizes the police to begin an investigation into the alleged offence.

What Happens Immediately After Registration?

Once an FIR is registered, the Investigating Officer is assigned to the case. The investigation may begin immediately depending on the nature and seriousness of the alleged offence.

The police may visit the place of occurrence, collect physical evidence, record witness statements, examine documents, review electronic evidence such as CCTV footage or mobile phone records, and prepare the necessary case documentation.

Every investigation aims to determine whether sufficient evidence exists to establish that an offence has actually been committed.

Does an FIR Mean Immediate Arrest?

One of the biggest misconceptions surrounding an FIR is that registration automatically results in arrest.

This is incorrect.

The Supreme Court of India has repeatedly emphasized that arrest is not mandatory in every criminal case. The police must exercise their powers in accordance with the law, considering factors such as the seriousness of the offence, the need for custodial interrogation, the possibility of tampering with evidence, and the likelihood of the accused fleeing from justice.

In many cases, individuals cooperate with the investigation without ever being arrested.

The Investigation Process

After registration of the FIR, the investigation generally follows a structured process.

The police collect evidence, examine witnesses, obtain expert opinions where necessary, record statements, and analyse all available material before reaching a conclusion.

Throughout this stage, the investigating agency is expected to remain objective. Their responsibility is to discover the truth rather than simply prove an allegation.

The investigation may ultimately support the complaint, partially support it, or reveal that no criminal offence has been committed.

Rights of the Complainant

The complainant has several important rights during the investigation.

These include the right to receive a copy of the FIR, to cooperate with the investigation, to provide additional information when required, and to be informed of significant developments in the case.

The complainant also has the opportunity to challenge certain decisions if dissatisfied with the outcome of the investigation.

Rights of the Accused

An FIR does not take away the constitutional rights of the accused.

Every individual continues to enjoy the presumption of innocence until proven guilty through a fair judicial process.

Depending upon the circumstances, the accused may seek anticipatory bail or regular bail, consult legal counsel, remain protected against self-incrimination, and receive a fair investigation conducted in accordance with law.

The criminal justice system is designed not only to investigate crime but also to protect individual liberty.

What Happens After the Investigation?

Once the investigation is completed, the police submit their findings before the competent court.

If sufficient evidence is available, a charge sheet is filed.

If the evidence is insufficient or no offence is established, the police may submit a closure report.

At this stage, the court independently examines the material before deciding how the proceedings should continue.

Common Misconceptions

Many people believe that an FIR automatically proves guilt.

It does not.

Others believe that every FIR results in arrest.

That is also incorrect.

Similarly, registration of an FIR does not mean that the case will necessarily end in conviction. Every allegation must pass through investigation, judicial scrutiny, and trial before any finding of guilt can be reached.

Understanding these distinctions helps reduce unnecessary fear and misinformation.

Conclusion

An FIR marks the beginning of a legal process—not its conclusion.

Whether you are a complainant seeking justice or an accused person protecting your rights, understanding what follows after registration of an FIR is essential.

The criminal justice system functions through investigation, evidence, judicial oversight, and due process. Knowledge of these stages enables individuals to approach the legal process with greater confidence rather than uncertainty.
